03 Definition

Cultural autophagy names the gesture by which a culture turns the process of critical digestion back upon itself. It no longer devours the Other in order to constitute identity, as in the modernist logic of Anthropophagy, but turns the gesture inward, digesting its own founding images, myths, symbolic repertoires, and devices of cultural memory.

The object of devouring is no longer external. It is the inheritance itself: the canon, the monument, the founding image, the cultural archive that both constitutes and paralyzes. Cultural autophagy is therefore not a celebration of identity, but a reflexive operation in which a culture examines what it has inherited and what that inheritance continues to demand from the present.

04 Genealogy

The concept emerges from a displacement of the Brazilian modernist paradigm of Anthropophagy, formulated by Oswald de Andrade in 1928. In Anthropophagy, the external element is devoured and transformed into local symbolic substance. In cultural autophagy, the vector is inverted: the culture turns upon its own archive and makes its own canon the object of digestion.

This displacement occurs in two movements. First, the object of devouring changes: it is no longer the European or the foreign Other, but the national, modern, and founding canon itself. Second, the movement no longer points toward synthesis or redemptive identity. Cultural autophagy diagnoses exhaustion where Anthropophagy once celebrated energy. It does not resolve the inheritance; it keeps it open as a field of critical introspection.

05 Distinguished from — anthropophagy and re-anthropophagy

Cultural autophagy is related to Anthropophagy, but it is not identical with it. Anthropophagy devours the Other in order to produce cultural transformation. Cultural autophagy devours the inherited self: its own icons, myths, images, and institutionalized narratives.

The concept also resonates with Indigenous re-anthropophagic operations, especially where modernist Anthropophagy is returned critically to the field of Indigenous authorship and historical violence. Yet cultural autophagy follows a different vector. It does not primarily reclaim Anthropophagy from an exterior historical position; it subjects the modernist icon to an internal digestion of its own canonization.

For this reason, the concept should not be treated as a synonym for Anthropophagy, re-anthropophagy, parody, homage, or appropriation. It names a specific inward turn: the moment in which a culture makes its own founding image the material of its interrogation.

06 Abaporu — operational instance

In Abaporu (2010), cultural autophagy manifests as an interrogation of the legacy of Tarsila do Amaral's Abaporu (1928). The artist's body occupies the position of the monumental figure from the painting, not in order to replicate it, but to expose the modernist icon to a new condition of embodiment, temporality, and critical reflection.

The body that reenacts does not simply reproduce. It performs the doubt surrounding the act of reenactment itself. In this operation, the modernist image is not rejected; it is recognized as a gravitational field and, at the same time, transformed into material for introspection. The work asks what happens when the myth of Anthropophagy itself becomes the food to be digested.

The application of the concept in Abaporu (2010) is an operational instance, not a limiting definition. Cultural autophagy may apply to other artistic practices in which a culture, tradition, or symbolic archive becomes the object of critical digestion from within.

07 Theory

Cultural autophagy belongs to the broader field of imagetic disappropriation. If imagetic disappropriation describes the operation by which an image detaches from its symbolic matrix to acquire autonomy, cultural autophagy names a specific case of that operation in which the matrix is not merely an external source, but the culture's own founding archive.

The concept also dialogues with hybrid dramaturgies and the auto-implicative method. It is not only an iconographic operation; it is also a performative and epistemological one. The artist's body becomes the site where cultural memory, modernist inheritance, canonization, irony, reverence, and critical distance coexist without synthesis.

In this sense, cultural autophagy is not a historical label imposed from outside the work. It is an operation performed by the image itself: the image metabolizes history, digests its own discourses, and returns them as critical fiction.

08 Scope note

The concept applies to artistic practices in which a culture, tradition, or symbolic archive becomes the object of critical reflection from within its own devices. It is transmedial: it may occur in photography, performance, video, sculpture, text, installation, or other artistic forms.

It does not apply to any simple reference to national culture, nor to decorative uses of canonical images. It requires an inward critical operation in which the inherited archive is not merely cited, but digested, displaced, and reactivated as a field of thought.

The concept does not depend on a single geographic context, although it emerges from the dialogue with Brazilian modernism and with the afterlife of Anthropophagy.
