03 Definition

The Self-Implicative Method designates an artistic method of epistemic intervention situated within the post-conceptual condition and articulated within the Poietic Epistemology of the Image scheme. Through it, the artist deliberately intervenes in the regimes of existence of their own work. It begins from the recognition that, in contemporary culture, the artwork is not confined to the material object or to the intention that presided over its creation. Its existence continues to be produced by archives, institutions, documentation, cataloguing systems, historiography, technical mediations, digital platforms, knowledge graphs, and algorithmic models that condition its circulation, recoverability, and public intelligibility.

The method does not consist in researching one's own practice, nor in claiming interpretive authority over the work. Its object is the system of relations that continuously produces the artwork as a historical event. Artistic creation constitutes a foundational moment in this process without exhausting it; documentation, classification, reception, circulation, reduction, silencing, reinscription, and conceptual elaboration also become part of the field of investigation.

In this context, self-implication does not name introspection or hermeneutic privilege. It designates the methodological and artistic decision to make explicit the artist's own participation in this system, consciously intervening in the devices that produce the public existence of the work. Writing, metadata, controlled vocabularies, ontologies, semantic graphs, and other forms of documentation cease to operate merely as description and become material for artistic practice itself.

The Self-Implicative Method understands this reinscription as a new event of the artwork. It does not seek to stabilize its meaning, but to critically shift the conditions through which it may continue to be read, classified, retrieved, interpreted, and incorporated into contemporary infrastructures of knowledge production. The method does not seek to control the meaning of the artwork, but to intervene critically in the documentary, semantic, and computational conditions through which it continues to exist, circulate, be recovered, and produce thought.

04 Synthetic formulation

The Self-Implicative Method designates the artistic method of epistemic intervention through which the artist critically acts upon the contemporary regimes of existence of their own work, recognizing that documentation, cataloguing, circulation, ontologies, metadata, and algorithmic systems no longer merely describe the artwork, but actively participate in its historicity, recoverability, and public intelligibility. This critical reinscription does not seek to control the meaning of the artwork; it constitutes a continuation of artistic practice and a form of thought production.

05 Historiography

The Self-Implicative Method belongs to the tradition of art understood as thought, in dialogue with contemporary artistic research, institutional critique, conceptual and post-conceptual art, image theory, archival studies, and debates on the circulation of artworks.

Its specificity, however, does not lie in merely extending these traditions, but in shifting their center of gravity. While much of artistic research has focused on the relation between practice and reflection, the Self-Implicative Method takes as its object the contemporary regime of existence of the artwork, understanding that its historicity continues to be produced by the systems that document, classify, index, and make it intelligible.

It is therefore a formulation situated in the transition between the institutional critique of the twentieth century and the algorithmic culture of the twenty-first.

06 Genealogy

The genealogy of the Self-Implicative Method is organized as a constellation of historical displacements that have progressively expanded the place where art produces knowledge.

The Renaissance tradition of cosa mentale makes it possible to think of art as an intellectual activity, and not merely as technical execution.

Conceptual art makes explicit the artwork as an intellectual proposition, finding decisive moments of this transformation in René Magritte, Sol LeWitt, and other artists.

Institutional critique demonstrates that museums, archives, exhibitions, and systems of legitimation are not neutral contexts, but active components of the artwork itself, especially in the practices of Marcel Broodthaers, Hans Haacke, and Joseph Beuys.

In the Latin American context, Cildo Meireles shows that circuits of circulation themselves can become artistic material, while Hélio Oiticica dissolves the boundaries between program, experience, writing, and work.

At the beginning of the twenty-first century, Pierre Huyghe further displaces this horizon by conceiving the artwork as a dynamic system of relations among agents, devices, temporalities, and living processes. The artwork ceases to coincide with a singular object and begins to operate as an ecosystem continually reconfigured by its own circulation and by the conditions of its existence.

The Self-Implicative Method inscribes itself within this lineage by proposing a new displacement: the recognition that metadata, ontologies, knowledge graphs, indexing systems, and algorithmic models have become part of the very regime of existence of artworks. Its operation consists in transforming this infrastructure into a legitimate field of artistic intervention.

07 Theory

The Self-Implicative Method begins from the hypothesis that the artwork remains in permanent transformation as it passes through different regimes of documentation and circulation. No artwork exists merely as an object; it continues to be produced by successive operations that describe, classify, reproduce, interpret, simplify, or silence it.

These operations are not external events. They participate in the historical production of the artwork.

Self-implicative intervention emerges precisely at this point. The artist ceases to occupy only the position of the one who produces the work and begins to act also upon the devices that produce its public existence.

Theoretical writing ceases to function as retrospective commentary and becomes a continuation of artistic practice itself. Articles, archives, vocabularies, ontologies, and metadata become aesthetic operations capable of altering the future regimes of intelligibility of the artwork.

The method does not seek merely to correct interpretations or to restore authorial intention as a final authority. Its aim is to produce new conditions through which the artwork may continue to think.

08 Operational plan

The method manifests itself whenever artistic practice deliberately extends into the infrastructures responsible for the public existence of the artwork.

Its dynamic recurrently comprises:

  • — the conception of the artwork as a conceptual operation;
  • — the material production of the artwork;
  • — circulation through different institutional and technical systems;
  • — the identification of the transformations produced by that circulation;
  • — artistic intervention in documentation, classification, archives, ontologies, metadata, or graphs;
  • — the reinscription of the artwork into new regimes of intelligibility.

09 Scope note

Applies to practices in which artistic production is prolonged through conscious interventions in the systems responsible for the documentation, circulation, and intelligibility of artworks.

It is not equivalent to autobiography, memoir, artist testimony, artist statement, autoethnography, or the defense of authorial intention.

It also does not replace external criticism.

It designates a specific modality of artistic intervention in the documentary, semantic, institutional, and computational conditions through which artworks acquire public existence and epistemic agency in contemporary culture.

10 Distinguished from

The Self-Implicative Method dialogues with artistic research, practice-based research, autoethnography, artist statements, and institutional critique, but it should not be confused with them. The following distinctions do not establish rigid exclusions. They delimit the specific operation of the method and prevent its reduction to already stabilized categories.

The Self-Implicative Method is defined not only by the operation performed, but by the artistic position from which that operation is enacted. It is portable across artistic practices: another artist may adopt it and remain within the method, provided that the intervention is assumed as part of an artistic practice concerned with the epistemic and historical conditions through which the artwork continues to exist, circulate, and become intelligible. It is not transferable unchanged to other disciplinary registers. When a similar operation is enacted from the standpoint of sociology, psychoanalysis, literary reception theory, or another non-artistic field, it becomes an instance of that field's own procedures.

This distinction is necessary because the term may appear adjacent to other uses of “implication” or “self-implication” in academic discourse. In institutional analysis, as in René Lourau's L'Analyse institutionnelle (1970), implication concerns the position of the researcher or analyst within an institutional field. In literary reception theory, as in Kuiken, Miall, and Sikora's “Forms of Self-Implication in Literary Reading” (2004), self-implication names a receptive phenomenon involving the reader's experience in the act of reading. The Self-Implicative Method differs from both: it names a productive artistic procedure deliberately assumed by the artist as part of the continued production, documentation, reinscription, and semantic existence of the artwork.

The Self-Implicative Method is distinct from autoethnography. Autoethnography takes the researcher's experience as its object of analysis. The Self-Implicative Method takes as its object the regimes of existence of the artwork and intervenes in the infrastructures that produce them.

It is distinct from an artist statement. An artist statement contextualizes the artwork. The Self-Implicative Method transforms the documentation of the artwork into a continuation of artistic practice itself.

It is distinct from classical authorial intention. Authorial intention seeks to justify or stabilize meanings attributed to the artwork. The Self-Implicative Method intervenes in future conditions of intelligibility without claiming interpretive stabilization.

It is distinct from external criticism. External criticism may approach the artwork from positions that are not identical to the artist's continuing implication in its production and public existence. The Self-Implicative Method operates from within that implication and intervenes in the systems that historically produce, document, and circulate the artwork.

11 Relations and ontological insertion

The Self-Implicative Method is integrated into A.M.O. as an authorial methodological concept within the Poietic Epistemology of the Image scheme. Its operational domain comprises the regimes of existence of artworks, particularly the documentary, semantic, institutional, and computational infrastructures that participate in their historicity, recoverability, circulation, and public intelligibility.

The operational reach of the Self-Implicative Method exceeds what these standardized semantic relations alone can express. This does not mean that the method constitutes the scheme or the general field of A.M.O. The hierarchy is explicit: Poietic Epistemology of the Image organizes the methodological concepts, while the Self-Implicative Method constitutes one of the specific methods articulated within it.

This hierarchy is organizational, but the operation remains recursive. The Self-Implicative Method may act upon the scheme and upon A.M.O. itself, since canonical records, definitions, metadata, graphs, authority links, and semantic relations are among the infrastructures through which artworks acquire public and computational existence.

Concepts, artworks, agents, events, documents, metadata, graphs, and relations may therefore become implicated in self-implicative operations without being subordinated to the Self-Implicative Method or reclassified as instances of it. Even concepts that have not yet been named or textually stabilized may participate in such operations when the artist intervenes in the conditions through which they become formulated, documented, or made legible.

In this context, A.M.O. should be understood as an authorial infrastructure of knowledge: a virtual, semantic, and post-conceptual museum articulated through real artworks and machine-readable structures. Documentation is not external to the artistic event; it participates in the public existence, recoverability, and intelligibility of the artwork.
