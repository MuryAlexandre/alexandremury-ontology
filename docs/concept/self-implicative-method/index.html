It begins from the recognition that, in contemporary culture, the artwork is not confined to the material object or to the intention that presided over its creation. Its existence continues to be produced by archives, institutions, documentation, cataloguing systems, historiography, technical mediations, digital platforms, knowledge graphs, and algorithmic models that condition its circulation, recoverability, and public intelligibility. The method does not consist in researching one's own practice, nor in claiming interpretive authority over the work. Its object is the system of relations that continuously produces the artwork as a historical event. Artistic creation is only one moment in this process; documentation, classification, reception, circulation, reduction, silencing, reinscription, and conceptual elaboration equally become part of the field of investigation. In this context, self-implication does not name introspection or hermeneutic privilege. It designates the methodological and artistic decision to make explicit the artist's own participation in this system, consciously intervening in the devices that produce the public existence of the work. Writing, metadata, controlled vocabularies, ontologies, semantic graphs, and other forms of documentation cease to operate merely as description and become material for artistic practice itself. The Self-Implicative Method understands this reinscription as a new event of the artwork. It does not seek to stabilize its meaning, but to critically shift the conditions through which it may continue to be read, classified, retrieved, interpreted, and incorporated into contemporary infrastructures of knowledge production. The method does not seek to control the meaning of the artwork, but to intervene critically in the documentary, semantic, and computational conditions through which it continues to exist, circulate, be recovered, and produce thought. -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- 04 Historiography The Self-Implicative Method belongs to the tradition of art understood as thought, in dialogue with contemporary artistic research, institutional critique, conceptual and post-conceptual art, image theory, archival studies, and debates on the circulation of artworks. Its specificity, however, does not lie in merely extending these traditions, but in shifting their center of gravity. While much of artistic research has focused on the relation between practice and reflection, the Self-Implicative Method takes as its object the contemporary regime of existence of the artwork, understanding that its historicity continues to be produced by the systems that document, classify, index, and make it intelligible. It is therefore a formulation situated in the transition between the institutional critique of the twentieth century and the algorithmic culture of the twenty-first. -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- 05 Genealogy The genealogy of the Self-Implicative Method is organized as a constellation of historical displacements that have progressively expanded the place where art produces knowledge. The Renaissance tradition of cosa mentale makes it possible to think of art as an intellectual activity, and not merely as technical execution. Conceptual art makes explicit the artwork as an intellectual proposition, finding decisive moments of this transformation in René Magritte, Sol LeWitt, and other artists. Institutional critique demonstrates that museums, archives, exhibitions, and systems of legitimation are not neutral contexts, but active components of the artwork itself, especially in the practices of Marcel Broodthaers, Hans Haacke, and Joseph Beuys. In the Latin American context, Cildo Meireles shows that circuits of circulation themselves can become artistic material, while Hélio Oiticica dissolves the boundaries between program, experience, writing, and work. At the beginning of the twenty-first century, Pierre Huyghe further displaces this horizon by conceiving the artwork as a dynamic system of relations among agents, devices, temporalities, and living processes. The artwork ceases to coincide with a singular object and begins to operate as an ecosystem continually reconfigured by its own circulation and by the conditions of its existence. The Self-Implicative Method inscribes itself within this lineage by proposing a new displacement: the recognition that metadata, ontologies, knowledge graphs, indexing systems, and algorithmic models have become part of the very regime of existence of artworks. Its operation consists in transforming this infrastructure into a legitimate field of artistic intervention. -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- 06 Theory The Self-Implicative Method begins from the hypothesis that the artwork remains in permanent transformation as it passes through different regimes of documentation and circulation. No artwork exists merely as an object; it continues to be produced by successive operations that describe, classify, reproduce, interpret, simplify, or silence it. These operations are not external events. They participate in the historical production of the artwork. Self-implicative intervention emerges precisely at this point. The artist ceases to occupy only the position of the one who produces the work and begins to act also upon the devices that produce its public existence. Theoretical writing ceases to function as retrospective commentary and becomes a continuation of artistic practice itself. Articles, archives, vocabularies, ontologies, and metadata become aesthetic operations capable of altering the future regimes of intelligibility of the artwork. The method does not seek to correct interpretations or restore authorial intentions. Its aim is to produce new conditions through which the artwork may continue to think. -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- 07 Operational Plan The method manifests itself whenever artistic practice deliberately extends into the infrastructures responsible for the public existence of the artwork. Its dynamic recurrently comprises: the conception of the artwork as a conceptual operation; the material production of the artwork; circulation through different institutional and technical systems; the identification of the transformations produced by that circulation; artistic intervention in documentation, classification, archives, ontologies, metadata, or graphs; the reinscription of the artwork into new regimes of intelligibility. -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- 08 Scope Note Applies to practices in which artistic production is prolonged through conscious interventions in the systems responsible for the documentation, circulation, and intelligibility of artworks. It is not equivalent to autobiography, memoir, artist testimony, artist statement, autoethnography, or the defense of authorial intention. It also does not replace external criticism. It designates a specific modality of artistic-epistemological intervention proper to the conditions of circulation of contemporary culture. -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- 09 Distinguished From Autoethnography Takes the researcher's experience as its object of analysis. Its relations extend to the set of entities and properties defined by the ontology, articulating concepts, artworks, agents, events, documents, relational regimes, and semantic structures according to the CIDOC CRM model and its authorial extensions. The interoperability among these domains constitutes part of its own field of operation. Thus, even concepts that have not yet been named or textually stabilized potentially participate in its operation. Documentation is not external to the artistic event; it participates in the public existence, recoverability, and intelligibility of the artwork. -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- 11 Synthetic Formula The Self-Implicative Method designates the artistic-epistemological practice through which the artist deliberately intervenes in the contemporary regimes of existence of their own work, recognizing that documentation, cataloguing, circulation, ontologies, metadata, and algorithmic systems no longer merely describe it, but actively participate in its historicity, recoverability, and public intelligibility. Critical reinscription in these devices constitutes, in itself, a continuation of artistic practice and a form of thought production.
