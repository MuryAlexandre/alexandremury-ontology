01 Definition

A.M.O. — Alexandre Mury Ontology is an authorial ontology and knowledge organization system developed by Alexandre Mury to document artworks, concepts, relational terms, terminology authorities, and semantic relations emerging from his artistic and theoretical practice.

It works in dialogue with CIDOC CRM, SKOS, Schema.org, JSON-LD, RDF, Wikidata, DOI-based scholarly circulation, and linked open data practices without replacing them.

CIDOC CRM supports documentary and museological description. SKOS organizes concepts and controlled vocabularies. Schema.org supports web discovery. JSON-LD supports machine-readable publication. A.M.O. provides an authorial vocabulary for describing relational, conceptual, documentary, and epistemological operations that may not be sufficiently specified by standard cultural heritage models.

The purpose of A.M.O. is to make artistic thought more legible and interoperable across human reading, cultural heritage documentation, linked open data, search engines, and artificial intelligence systems, without prescribing a single interpretation of the works.

02 What A.M.O. does not do

A.M.O. does not prescribe a single interpretation of the works.

A.M.O. does not replace CIDOC CRM, SKOS, Schema.org, Wikidata, DOI records, museum records, scholarly references, or external criticism.

A.M.O. does not convert artistic practice into a closed theoretical doctrine.

A.M.O. does not treat external sources, interlocutors, genealogies, or analogies as the authorial origin of its concepts.

A.M.O. does not require every future artwork, relation, or concept to be manually listed on this page in order to be interoperable. Each record is identified by its own canonical URI and carries its own human-readable page and machine-readable metadata.

03 Purpose

The A.M.O. namespace was created to provide stable, persistent, machine-readable identifiers for artworks, concepts, relational operations, documents, persons, and external anchoring nodes associated with Alexandre Mury's artistic and theoretical practice.

The subdomain https://id.alexandremury.art/ functions as a cataloguing, museological, semantic, and machine-readable layer.

It is distinct from the visual and public-facing website https://www.alexandremury.art/, which remains oriented toward broader human presentation.

This page functions as a matrix document. It preserves the conceptual, terminological, and technical decisions that organize A.M.O. across human-readable pages, JSON-LD records, RDF-compatible structures, CIDOC CRM descriptions, SKOS concepts, and Schema.org metadata.

05 Publication model

A.M.O. records are structured as paired canonical records: a human-readable index.html and a corresponding machine-readable metadata.jsonld.

Each canonical record may describe a concept, artwork, person, scheme, relational term, external anchoring node, document, event, material manifestation, digital object, or semantic relation.

The ontology remains extensible. New records, terms, artworks, relations, schemes, or external anchoring nodes may be added, renamed, reorganized, or withdrawn as the system develops, without altering the general namespace policy or the autonomy of already published records.

Aggregate distributions such as ontology.jsonld, concepts.jsonld, RDF/Turtle files, and CIDOC CRM serializations may be generated from the canonical records when appropriate.

06 Layers

06.1 Documentary layer

The documentary layer uses CIDOC CRM to describe agents, events, identifiers, rights, documents, collections, institutional relations, material manifestations, and records associated with artworks.

In this layer, a material manifestation may be modeled as crm:E22 Human-Made Object, a production process as crm:E12 Production, a performative realization as crm:E7 Activity, and a documentary or digital record as crm:E31 Document or crm:E73 Information Object.

Post-conceptual artworks may require layered modeling. A canonical artwork record should not be reduced automatically to a single physical object, support, image file, event, concept, or document.

06.2 Conceptual layer

The conceptual layer uses SKOS, Schema.org, CIDOC CRM, and A.M.O. properties to document authorial concepts as independent conceptual entities.

These concepts are not merely keywords. They have identity, definition, scope, authorship, documentation, and the possibility of reuse in other contexts.

06.3 Relational terms

Relational terms qualify modes of relation between artworks, images, bodies, figures, iconographic traditions, symbolic archives, and pre-existing cultural signs.

They do not prescribe interpretation. They describe modes of relation.

06.4 Terminology authority layer

The terminology authority layer registers authorized forms, original labels, language of origin, canonical slugs, authorial coinage, publication sources, Wikidata alignment, DOI circulation, and indexing strategy.

06.5 Machine-readable layer

The machine-readable layer distributes semantic information through JSON-LD and other RDF-compatible structures. Each canonical record carries its own metadata.jsonld, allowing human-readable documentation and machine-readable description to remain synchronized without overloading the visual page.

09 Relation between artworks and concepts

A.M.O. distinguishes between an artwork, a concept, and the relation between them.

An artwork may mobilize one or more concepts. A concept may be mobilized by one or more artworks. Neither relation exhausts the meaning of the work or the concept.

Preferred relation
amo:mobilizesConcept

Related relation
amo:hasOperationalInstance

Use principle
An artwork may mobilize a concept without being reduced to that concept. A concept may have an operational instance without being limited by that instance.

10 Relational terms

Relational terms describe how artworks relate to prior images, figures, traditions, archives, symbolic structures, or cultural signs.

They are not interpretive commands. They do not determine how a work must be read. Their function is to name relational operations that may be present in a work without exhausting its meaning.

Portuguese category name
Regimes de Relação

English category name
Relational regimes / Modes of relation

Preferred property

Relational terms may include operations such as reenactment, self-representation, visual citation, internal critical digestion, metadata activation, polysemic articulation, and other modes of relation. This list is intentionally not exhaustive.

11 External anchoring nodes

External anchoring nodes are used to identify interlocutors, genealogies, analogies, and conceptual sources that are external to A.M.O.

They are not authorial concepts of A.M.O.

Their function is to preserve attribution, distinguish genealogical references from authorial formulation, and support semantic alignment with external sources.

URI pattern
https://id.alexandremury.art/external/{external-node-slug}/

Example
https://id.alexandremury.art/external/disappropriazione-agamben/

Use principle
External references may inform a field of dialogue without becoming the origin, equivalent, or synonym of an A.M.O. concept.

12 Terminology authority

A.M.O. maintains a terminology authority layer for authorial concepts, relational terms, external anchoring nodes, and machine-readable records.

This layer preserves:

  • — canonical English slug;
  • — original Portuguese label;
  • — multilingual labels;
  • — language of origin;
  • — intellectual authorship;
  • — publication source;
  • — DOI when available;
  • — Wikidata alignment when available;
  • — indexing strategy;
  • — distinction between authorial source and field of dialogue.

Terminology principle
The English URI slug supports interoperability. The Portuguese original label preserves conceptual genealogy and authorial formulation.

13 Indexing strategy

A.M.O. adopts an intentional bilingual register.

English supports international indexing, RDF/Linked Data circulation, Wikidata alignment, search-engine retrieval, and machine-readable processing.

Portuguese preserves the conceptual origin, linguistic specificity, and authorial genealogy of the terms.

The two registers do not compete. They operate in different computational and discursive environments while referring to the same conceptual entities.

Indexing goal
To make concepts, artworks, and semantic relations discoverable by humans, cataloguing systems, knowledge graphs, search engines, and artificial intelligence systems without sacrificing conceptual specificity.
