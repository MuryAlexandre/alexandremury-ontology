01 Definition

A.M.O. — Alexandre Mury Ontology is an authorial ontology and knowledge organization system developed by Alexandre Mury to document artworks, concepts, Regimes of Relation, terminology authorities, and semantic relations emerging from his artistic and theoretical practice.

It works in dialogue with CIDOC CRM, SKOS, Schema.org, JSON-LD, RDF, Wikidata, DOI-based scholarly circulation, and linked open data practices without replacing them. CIDOC CRM supports the documentary and museological description of artworks, agents, events, collections, rights, records, and institutional relations; SKOS organizes concepts and controlled vocabularies; Schema.org supports web discovery; JSON-LD supports machine-readable publication; and A.M.O. provides an authorial vocabulary for describing relational and conceptual operations that may not be sufficiently specified by standard cultural heritage models.

The purpose of A.M.O. is to make artistic thought more legible and interoperable across human reading, cultural heritage documentation, linked open data, search engines, and artificial intelligence systems, without prescribing a single interpretation of the works.

02 What A.M.O. does not do

A.M.O. does not prescribe a single interpretation of the works.

A.M.O. does not replace CIDOC CRM, SKOS, Schema.org, Wikidata, DOI records, museum records, or scholarly references.

A.M.O. does not convert artistic practice into a closed theoretical doctrine.

A.M.O. does not treat external sources, interlocutors, genealogies, or analogies as the authorial origin of its concepts.

A.M.O. does not reduce artworks to metadata. Rather, it treats metadata, documentation, concepts, relations, and machine-readable descriptions as part of the expanded documentary life of the work.

03 Purpose

The A.M.O. namespace was created to provide stable, persistent, machine-readable identifiers for artworks, concepts, relational operations, documents, persons, and external anchoring nodes associated with Alexandre Mury's artistic and theoretical practice.

The subdomain https://id.alexandremury.art/ functions as a cataloguing, museological, semantic, and machine-readable layer. It is distinct from the visual and public-facing website https://www.alexandremury.art/, which remains oriented toward broader human presentation.

This page functions as a matrix document. It preserves the conceptual, terminological, and technical decisions required for later distribution into separate JSON-LD, RDF/Turtle, CIDOC CRM, and SKOS files. At the present stage, it intentionally centralizes information in one human-verifiable document in order to reduce fragmentation and avoid inconsistent parallel versions.

06 Layers

06.1 Documentary layer

The documentary layer uses CIDOC CRM to describe artworks, agents, events, titles, identifiers, rights, documents, collections, and institutional relations. It is responsible for museum-oriented and heritage-compatible documentation.

In this layer, an artwork such as Abaporu (2010) may be modeled as a crm:E22 Human-Made Object, its creation as crm:E12 Production, its performatic realization as crm:E7 Activity, and its documentation as crm:E31 Document.

06.2 Conceptual layer

The conceptual layer uses SKOS and CIDOC CRM to document authorial concepts as independent conceptual entities. These concepts are not keywords. They have identity, definition, scope, authorship, documentation, and the possibility of reuse in other contexts.

06.3 Regimes of Relation

Regimes of Relation qualify modes of relation between artworks, images, figures, iconographic traditions, symbolic archives, and pre-existing cultural signs.

They do not prescribe interpretation. They describe relational operations.

Examples include reinterpretation, visual citation, intertextuality, reenactment, and self-representation.

06.4 Terminology authority layer

The terminology authority layer registers authorized forms, original labels, language of origin, canonical slugs, authorial coinage, publication sources, Wikidata alignment, DOI circulation, and indexing strategy.

06.5 Machine-readable layer

The machine-readable layer will later distribute the ontology through JSON-LD, RDF/Turtle, and related formats. In the current phase, core machine-readable metadata remains attached to individual records and to this page through embedded JSON-LD.

09 Regimes of Relation

Regimes of Relation are controlled vocabulary terms that describe how artworks relate to prior images, figures, traditions, archives, symbolic structures, or cultural signs.

They are not interpretive commands. They do not determine how a work must be read. Their function is to name relational operations that may be present in a work without exhausting its meaning.

10 Artworks

Artwork records are modeled through a combination of Schema.org, CIDOC CRM, and A.M.O. properties. They connect material documentation, performatic processes, conceptual operations, museum collections, authority identifiers, and machine-readable metadata.

Abaporu (2010)
URI: https://id.alexandremury.art/artworks/abaporu-2010/
Status: published canonical artwork record

Abaporu (2010) is not merely an example of a concept. Within A.M.O., it functions as an artwork record, a material and performatic entity, and an operational instance through which concepts such as cultural autophagy and imagetic disappropriation become legible. The concepts, however, are not limited to this work. They remain autonomous and may be applied, recombined, or tested in relation to other artworks and practices.

11 Relation between artworks and concepts

A.M.O. distinguishes between an artwork, a concept, and the relation between them.

An artwork may mobilize one or more concepts. A concept may be mobilized by one or more artworks. Neither relation exhausts the meaning of the work or the concept.

For this reason, A.M.O. avoids forcing artworks into standard properties when those properties are not precise enough. In such cases, authorial extension properties may be used with caution, always preserving compatibility with the documentary layer.

Preferred relation
amo:mobilizesConcept

Related relation
amo:hasOperationalInstance

Use principle
An artwork may mobilize a concept without being reduced to that concept. A concept may have an operational instance without being limited by that instance.

12 External anchoring nodes

External anchoring nodes are used to identify interlocutors, genealogies, analogies, and conceptual sources that are external to A.M.O.

They are not authorial concepts of A.M.O.

Their function is to preserve attribution, distinguish genealogical references from authorial formulation, and support semantic alignment with external sources.

Example
Disappropriazione — Giorgio Agamben

Use principle
External references may inform a field of dialogue without becoming the origin, equivalent, or synonym of an A.M.O. concept.

13 Terminology authority

A.M.O. maintains a terminology authority layer for authorial concepts, Regimes of Relation, external anchoring nodes, and future machine-readable records.

This layer preserves:

  • — canonical English slug;
  • — original Portuguese label;
  • — multilingual labels;
  • — language of origin;
  • — intellectual authorship;
  • — publication source;
  • — DOI when available;
  • — Wikidata alignment when available;
  • — indexing strategy;
  • — distinction between authorial source and field of dialogue.

Terminology principle
The English URI slug supports interoperability. The Portuguese original label preserves conceptual genealogy and authorial formulation.

14 Indexing strategy

A.M.O. adopts an intentional bilingual register.

English supports international indexing, RDF/Linked Data circulation, Wikidata alignment, search-engine retrieval, and machine-readable processing.

Portuguese preserves the conceptual origin, linguistic specificity, and authorial genealogy of the terms.

The two registers do not compete. They operate in different computational and discursive environments while referring to the same conceptual entities.

Indexing goal
To make concepts, artworks, and semantic relations discoverable by humans, cataloguing systems, knowledge graphs, search engines, and artificial intelligence systems without sacrificing conceptual specificity.
