Lisa (2013) ALEXANDRE MURY Canonical URI: https://id.alexandremury.art/artworks/lisa-2013/ -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- DESCRIPTION Lisa is a hybrid artwork created by Alexandre Mury in 2013, unfolding between videoperformance, video art, performative self-portraiture, photography, body art, and historical citation. Conceived in dialogue with Leonardo da Vinci's Mona Lisa, also known as La Gioconda, the work does not operate as a simple restaging of a canonical image, but as a temporal, corporeal, and critical activation of one of the most recognizable portraits in the history of art. Lisa was intentionally conceived and exhibited in vertical format, with a 9:16 screen ratio, to be displayed on a rotated monitor. This technical choice is not incidental: it displaces the usual horizontal orientation of digital moving images and reclaims the verticality of the classical portrait canvas. The video therefore does not merely document an action; it transforms the moving image itself into a portrait format. At the center of the work is a sustained performative action in which the artist, positioned in a composition that evokes the Mona Lisa, gradually removes facial and bodily signs associated with gender, identity, and historical representation. The act of shaving eyebrows, beard, and hair becomes a temporal incision into the portrait. What appears at first as a recognizable citation of Leonardo's painting is progressively destabilized by the vulnerability of the body, the friction of the blade, the exposed skin, and the transformation of the artist's own image. The work is grounded in a precise tension between stillness and duration. Where the Renaissance portrait seeks permanence, Lisa introduces process. Where the painted image offers the fiction of an eternal face, the videoperformance insists on alteration, discomfort, and metamorphosis. Through this movement, the artwork turns the portrait into an event and the icon into a field of corporeal negotiation. The title Lisa operates through an intentional ambiguity. It refers to Mona Lisa, while also playing with the Portuguese word "lisa", meaning smooth or shaved. This linguistic displacement is central to the work. The title does not merely name the image; it activates the action that produces it. The body becomes "lisa" through a gesture that is at once comic, painful, jocose, violent, regenerative, and self-implicative. The video includes a textual and historical layer through the use of an excerpt from Giorgio Vasari's Lives of the Artists, in which Vasari describes Leonardo's portrait of Mona Lisa as an extraordinary imitation of life. By bringing this Renaissance discourse into the field of contemporary videoperformance, the work creates a historical friction between art as mental construction, art as bodily experience, and art as a living process of reinterpretation. The soundtrack, Antonio Vivaldi's La Stravaganza, Concerto No. 2 RV 279, introduces another temporal layer. Renaissance portraiture, Baroque music, digital video, performance, photography, and contemporary self-representation are not organized as linear references, but as overlapping regimes of time. Lisa thus operates as a hybrid dramaturgy: a work in which image, body, voice, music, citation, duration, and documentation produce meaning together. The photographic version of Lisa should not be understood as separate from the videoperformance, nor should the video be reduced to a making-of of the photograph. Both belong to the same artistic operation. The photograph condenses the performative event into a still image, while the video exposes the temporal, bodily, and procedural dimension through which the image comes into being. Together, they form a hybrid artwork in which photography and moving image are mutually implicated. Produced in the context of contemporary Brazilian art, Lisa participates in a broader field of critical rereadings of canonical images. Its dialogue with Leonardo da Vinci is not limited to appropriation or visual quotation. The work interrogates the persistence of historical images, the instability of identity, the performativity of gender, the fragility of authorship, and the way inherited images continue to act upon contemporary bodies. More than a reinterpretation of Mona Lisa, Lisa is a work about the conditions through which images survive. It asks what happens when a canonical portrait is no longer approached as an untouchable object, but as a living structure capable of being crossed by body, irony, pain, desire, gender, history, and time. In this sense, Lisa becomes simultaneously video, photograph, performance, document, portrait, citation, and critical fiction. -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- TECHNICAL DATA Title: Lisa Artist: Alexandre Mury Year: 2013 Medium: Videoperformance; video art; digital photography; performative self-portrait Duration: 4'05" Screen ratio: 9:16 vertical format Display mode: Rotated monitor / vertical orientation Genre: Videoperformance; video art; hybrid artwork; performative self-portrait; body art Photography, direction, script, and voice: Alexandre Mury Editing: João Henrique Costa Textual source: Excerpt from Giorgio Vasari, Lives of the Artists Music: Antonio Vivaldi, La Stravaganza, Concerto No. 2 RV 279 Related artwork: Leonardo da Vinci, Mona Lisa / La Gioconda, 1503–1519 -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- EXHIBITIONS & RECOGNITION Fricções Históricas, solo exhibition, Caixa Cultural Rio de Janeiro Fricções Históricas, solo exhibition, SESC Glória PIPA Prize, nominated, 2016 — premiopipa.com/alexandre-mury -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- SOURCES & PROVENANCE Own publication: Alexandre Mury, "Desapropriação Teórica: Ficção Crítica e Metanoia Hermenêutica da Imagem," Zenodo, 2025–2026. The work presents the artist in a frontal or three-quarter portrait composition that evokes Leonardo da Vinci's Mona Lisa. The figure appears seated before a painted landscape background, with crossed hands and a controlled pose that recalls the classical portrait format. During the video, the artist performs a bodily transformation through shaving and depilation, removing facial hair, eyebrows, and hair while facing the camera and the mirror. The action introduces tension between the stillness of the Renaissance portrait and the duration of contemporary performance. The work uses vertical 9:16 format, displayed on a rotated monitor, reinforcing its relationship to the verticality of classical portraiture. Through body art, video, photography, historical citation, music, and self-representation, Lisa reinterprets the Mona Lisa as a living process of identity, gender, image, and temporal transformation. -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- ← Back to Artworks © Alexandre Mury --------------------------------------------------------------------------------
